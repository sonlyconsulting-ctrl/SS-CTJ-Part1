WEEK 1: FOUNDATIONS OF LOGIC

Day 1 | What Is Logic?

Theme Overview: Logic is the structured framework that separates fact from opinion and belief from proof, improving decision-making in daily life.

Mini-Framework: IF-THEN Statements: Break situations into cause-and-effect relationships.

🧠 Logic Tip: Logic isn’t about being right; it’s about being consistent and clear. Example: “IF I wake early, THEN I’m more productive.”

Visual Aid: Describe a real-life situation where you could apply an IF-THEN flowchart. This could be a personal decision, a simple work process, or even how you troubleshoot a common issue. Outline the 'IF (condition)' and 'THEN (outcome)' steps in plain language. Reflect on how breaking it down like this helps clarify the situation. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write your belief about what logic means to you personally.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using IF-THEN Statements, outline a recent decision. Identify conditions (IF) and outcomes (THEN). What evidence supported these connections?

Response Area: Between 75–100 words

🎯 Prompt 3: For a complex situation you face, how would you use IF-THEN to clarify elements or solutions?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Reflect on: “The unexamined life is not worth living.” —Socrates. How does this relate to logic?

Response Area: 10-word summary + 100-word explanation

Dig Deeper: Explain logic to a 10-year-old using a daily example.

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

Day 2 | Thinking in Statements

Theme Overview: Thoughts are often unspoken statements. Converting them to clear, testable statements refines reasoning.

Mini-Framework: Declarative Sentences: Use “I think... because...” to structure reasoning.

🧠 Logic Tip: Every belief can be a statement; every statement can be tested.

Visual Aid: Describe a belief you hold about a daily situation (e.g., a relationship, a work task, a personal habit). Outline the 'I think... because...' structure of your reasoning. Reflect on how articulating this helps clarify the belief and its foundations. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write a strong belief about work, relationships, or life.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using Declarative Sentences, convert your belief into a statement. What experiences or data support it? What counter-arguments could weaken it?

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: How might personal experiences or biases influence your statement’s framing or evidence?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Convert your belief into a syllogism (e.g., All A are B; C is A; C is B).

Response Area: Syllogism + 100-word explanation

Today’s Challenge: Diagram your thought process using boxes and arrows.

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

Day 3 | Truth vs Belief

Theme Overview: Truth exists independently; belief is what we think exists. This distinction sharpens reasoning.

Mini-Framework: Logic Ladder: Start with a fact, add assumptions, then conclusions. Example: “Fact: It’s raining. Assumption: I’ll get wet. Conclusion: Take an umbrella.”

🧠 Logic Tip: Ask: “Do I believe this because it’s true, or think it’s true because I believe it?”

Visual Aid: Choose a personal belief or a common assumption you encounter. Outline the 'Logic Ladder' for it: identify a foundational 'Fact', the 'Assumption(s)' built upon it, and the resulting 'Conclusion'. Reflect on how clearly separating these elements impacts your understanding. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write a strong belief you can’t prove with certainty.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using the Logic Ladder, identify facts or assumptions leading to your belief’s conclusion. What evidence supports each step?

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: How might distinguishing truth from belief have changed a past decision?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Reflect on: “Belief does not make truth.” —Voltaire. How does this relate to truth vs belief?

Response Area: 10-word summary + 100-word explanation

Dig Deeper: Compare your view today with a year ago on this topic.

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

Day 4 | Deductive & Inductive Reasoning

Theme Overview: Deductive reasoning moves from general principles to specific conclusions; inductive reasoning builds principles from observations. Both are key to logical thinking.

Mini-Framework: Reasoning Type Identifier: Determine if your reasoning is inductive (observations → pattern) or deductive (principle → conclusion).

🧠 Logic Tip: Deductive reasoning ensures certainty if premises are true; inductive reasoning offers probability.

Visual Aid: Describe a situation where you used either inductive or deductive reasoning. If inductive, list the specific 'Observations' that led to a 'Pattern'. If deductive, state the general 'Principle' and the 'Conclusion' derived from it. Reflect on how identifying the type of reasoning helps evaluate its strength. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write about a pattern you’ve noticed in life or work.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using the Reasoning Type Identifier, is your pattern inductive (list observations) or deductive (state principle)?

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: How might biases influence your interpretation of observations or principles?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Predict an outcome based on your pattern. Explain your reasoning.

Response Area: Prediction + 100-word explanation

Today’s Challenge: Ask someone’s opinion on your pattern and why.

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

Day 5 | The Power of a Premise

Theme Overview: Premises are the starting points of reasoning. Weak premises lead to flawed conclusions, no matter how logical the process.

Mini-Framework: Point-Evidence-Reason (PER Method): Make a claim, support with data, explain why it matters.

🧠 Logic Tip: Question your premises; conclusions are only as strong as the weakest link.

Visual Aid: Think of a recent confident decision you made. Describe it using the Point-Evidence-Reason (PER) method: state your 'Point' (decision), the 'Evidence' that supported it, and the underlying 'Reason' (premise). Reflect on how clearly defining your premises strengthens your decision-making. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write a recent confident decision.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using PER, state your decision (Point), evidence, and premise (Reason).

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: If a premise proved false, how would your reasoning change? What new premises might you explore?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Challenge one premise. How does it alter your conclusion?

Response Area: Premise + 100-word explanation

Dig Deeper: Connect premises to a current news event.

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

WEEK 1 CHECKPOINT

Reflect on your progress in building logical foundations. Which mini-framework was most helpful, and why? How has your understanding of logic evolved?

Response Area: Between 100–150 words

WEEK 2: BIAS & FALLACIES

Day 6 | What Is Bias?

Theme Overview: Bias is the unconscious tendency to favor certain perspectives, clouding judgment. Recognizing biases is key to clearer thinking.

Mini-Framework: What if... So what... Now what? Explore implications, consequences, actions.

🧠 Logic Tip: Don’t eliminate bias; recognize when it affects your judgment.

Visual Aid: Describe a situation where you suspect bias might have influenced your perception or a decision. Outline what you initially 'Believed' (biased side) versus the broader 'Facts' that might offer a different perspective. Reflect on how recognizing this potential imbalance helps you seek a more objective view. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write about a time you realized you were wrong about something important.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: What if you realized earlier? What consequences or actions would change? What facts led to your shift?

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: Where did preconceptions shape your interpretation of facts?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Reflect on: “We see the world not as it is, but as we are.” —Anaïs Nin. How does this relate to bias?

Response Area: 10-word summary + 100-word explanation

Dig Deeper: Explain your realization to invite discussion.

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

Day 7 | Confirmation Bias

Theme Overview: Confirmation bias favors information that supports existing beliefs, ignoring contradictions. It’s a common thinking trap.

Mini-Framework: Seek Disconfirming Evidence: Actively challenge your opinions.

🧠 Logic Tip: Strong arguments withstand scrutiny; seek counter-evidence.

Visual Aid: Recall a strong opinion you hold. Describe the type of 'Counter-Evidence' you would actively seek to challenge it. Reflect on how deliberately searching for disconfirming information strengthens (or modifies) your understanding, rather than just confirming existing views. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write a strong opinion on politics, religion, or social issues.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: List reasons and sources supporting your opinion. Identify weaknesses or counter-arguments.

Response Area: Between 50–75 words]

🎯 Prompt 3: How would you seek disconfirming evidence before forming this opinion?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Reflect on: “The beginning of wisdom is calling things by their right names.” —Confucius. How does precision affect conclusions?

Response Area: 10-word summary + 100-word explanation

Today’s Challenge: Describe your thought process with imagery or metaphors.

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

Day 8 | Availability Heuristic

Theme Overview: The availability heuristic overestimates likelihood based on easily recalled examples, skewing perception of probability.

Mini-Framework: Evidence Balancer: List evidence for and against your fear/concern.

🧠 Logic Tip: Easy-to-remember events aren’t necessarily more likely.

Visual Aid: Think of a fear or concern that feels very real to you due to easily recalled examples (e.g., news stories, personal anecdotes). Outline the 'Memories' that make it feel prominent versus the broader 'Facts' or statistics that might offer a different perspective. Reflect on how the availability of information can skew your perception of probability. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write a fear or concern that feels very real now.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using Evidence Balancer, list evidence supporting and countering your fear.

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: How might recent experiences exaggerate this concern?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Convert your fear into an IF-THEN statement and test its logic.

Response Area: IF-THEN statement + 100-word explanation

Dig Deeper: Compare your view today with a year ago.

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

Day 9 | Ad Hominem & Straw Man

Theme Overview: Ad hominem attacks the person, not the argument; straw man misrepresents positions. Both derail reasoning.

Mini-Framework: Argument Focus: Concentrate on ideas, not people, and represent views fairly.

🧠 Logic Tip: Attack ideas, not people; fairly challenge opposing views.

Visual Aid: Describe a heated discussion you've witnessed or participated in. Outline how you would reframe the conversation to focus solely on the 'Idea' or argument, rather than attacking the 'Person' or misrepresenting their 'Position'. Reflect on how this shift promotes more constructive dialogue. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write about a heated disagreement.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: Using Argument Focus, identify key ideas. Did anyone attack the person or misrepresent positions?

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: What’s the first logical step to clarify the disagreement? How might this change your perspective?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Reflect on: “To understand is to forgive, even oneself.” —Alexander Chase. How does this relate to avoiding personal attacks?

Response Area: 10-word summary + 100-word explanation

Today’s Challenge: Ask someone’s opinion on the disagreement and why.

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

Day 10 | False Dilemmas & Red Herrings

Theme Overview: False dilemmas present only two options when more exist; red herrings distract with irrelevant information. Both limit clear thinking.

Mini-Framework: Declarative Sentences: State your “two-option” choice as “I think X because...”. Identify a third option or red herring.

🧠 Logic Tip: Look for unmentioned third options when told “either-or.”

Visual Aid: Recall a situation where you felt presented with only 'Option 1' or 'Option 2'. Describe these two options and then brainstorm a viable 'Alternative' or a 'Red Herring' that might have distracted from other possibilities. Reflect on how identifying false dilemmas opens up new solutions. Response Area: Describe your visual aid (up to 100 words)

💡 Prompt 1: Write a choice with seemingly only two options.

Response Area: Up to 25 words

✏️ Prompt 2: State your reasoning for the two options. Imagine a third option or scenario where neither applies. What evidence supports or weakens the two-option view?

Response Area: Between 50–75 words

🎯 Prompt 3: Does your background make you favor one option unconsciously?

Response Area: Between 50–100 words

🔄 Bonus: Identify a third option and explain its viability.

Response Area: Third option + 100-word explanation

Dig Deeper: Connect this theme to a news event.

Response Area: Between 50–100 words
